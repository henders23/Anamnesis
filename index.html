Anamnesis
Build a Memory Palace
Turn your next talk into a place you can walk through

How it works

  1. Draw rooms on the drafting paper. Draw one against another and a doorway appears on its own.
  2. Furnish them — hang pictures, raise statues, write on the floor. Each object carries a point of your talk.
  3. Set the route — every picture and statue joins a numbered path in the order you'll speak.
  4. Explore — step inside and walk the palace in first person, following the golden trail from point to point.

Why a palace?

Anamnesis (Greek ἀνάμνησις, “recollection”) revives the ancient method of loci — the memory palace of classical orators, who pinned each part of a speech to a place in an imagined building and walked it in the mind's eye. We remember places and journeys far more readily than lists, so anchoring each point of an argument to a room and an object makes a talk genuinely hard to forget.

The technique is first set down in the anonymous Rhetorica ad Herennium (c. 80 BCE) and retold in Cicero's De Oratore; see Frances Yates, The Art of Memory (1966). Modern imaging confirms it works: memory champions rely on spatial routes rather than raw talent (Maguire et al., Nature Neuroscience, 2003), and a few weeks of loci training reshapes ordinary memory networks (Dresler et al., Neuron, 2017).

Make it yours

Please change the title and author. Top-left of the screen you'll find the presentation title (“An Unforgettable Argument”) and a byline reading by Your Name. Click either to type your own — they're saved with your palace when you export it.

Design & download your own

Everything is built in Design mode using the tools down the left edge, then previewed in Explore. When you're happy, hit Export in the top bar to download your palace as a self-contained .html file you can keep, share, or simply open in any browser to walk it again — or re-load it here with Import.
